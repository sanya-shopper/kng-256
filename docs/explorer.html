SHA-256 as a Mathematical Object

The machine, running: a round-by-round explorer

This page runs the actual \(\SHA\) compression function on a message you type, and lets you watch the internal state ripple, one round at a time. The format follows a small genre of excellent visualizers—sha256algorithm.com, the terminal sha256-animation, the visual SHA-256 calculator—which show the eight working registers as rows of bits, shifting and recombining every step. What we add is the paper's lens: every operation is colored by which of the two rival geometries of §3.2 it respects PDF p. 12.

The color code. Wine marks the \(\Ftwo\)-linear operations (\(\shaxor\), rotations, shifts): isometries of the Hamming cube, transparent to linear algebra. Navy marks addition \(\shaplus\) modulo \(2^{32}\): continuous in the \(2\)-adic metric, opaque to \(\Ftwo\)-linear eyes because of carries. Green marks the genuinely nonlinear boolean votes \(\mathrm{Ch}\) and \(\mathrm{Maj}\). Wherever a \(\shaplus\) appears, the carry residue row shows \((x \shaplus y) \shaxor (x \shaxor y)\): the exact bits where the two geometries disagree—the coupling the whole design leans on (§7.1 PDF p. 44).

Sixty-four rounds: the state ripples PDF p. 16

The eight registers \(a,\dots,h\) start at the fractional parts of \(\sqrt{2},\dots,\sqrt{19}\) (§3.6). Each round assembles \(T_1\) and \(T_2\) from the colored ingredients below, then shifts every register down one slot—only two slots (\(a\) and \(e\)) receive genuinely new material per round; highlighted bits are the ones that just changed. Step, scrub, or let it play. The circuit diagram shows the round's dataflow with this round's actual values riding the wires; the bit table below it shows the same computation bit by bit.

One flipped bit, two metrics PDF p. 12

Run the machine twice, on your message and on a twin with a single bit flipped, and chart the difference round by round. This is the avalanche of the §3 demo under a microscope—and a concrete taste of why the two geometries of §3.2 are both blind once diffusion saturates. Reduced-round attacks (§7.4) live entirely in the left margin of this chart, before the blue curve reaches its ceiling.

The feedforward, and the digest PDF p. 18

After round 63 the machine has computed a bijection of the state. The digest is not that bijection's output: Davies–Meyer adds the initial value back in, register by register (§3.4)—the single step that makes the map lossy and one-way, as §5.3 explains (“unless you delete the \(\shaplus\)” PDF p. 29).
